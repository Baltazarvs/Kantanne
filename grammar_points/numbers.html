Japanese Numbers

Japanese numbers are quite different from English and can be complex when it comes to usage. Complexity is mainly due to:

  • Two different readings
  • Counters
Contents:
  • Introduction
  • Japanese Reading
  • Sino-Japanese Reading

Introduction

The most important thing about number complexity in Japanese is two different ways to read numbers and learning when to use which.

It is obvious that each 訓読み (kunyomi) character is suffixed with つ. It is general-purpose counter that is exclusive to Japanese read numberals. Life would be by far easier if these native Japanese numbers were used only, but then we have numbers that use Sino-Japanese reading. They are by far more complex in terms of usage as many different counters are attached to them instead of Japanese ones and, even more troublesome, Japanese read numbers are limited to 10 when it comes to counting.

例

Japanese Reading

As already said, Japanese read numbers are limited to 10 and they use general-purpose counter つ. When they are used for counting, they are mostly used after thing they are counting. It means that they usually go after object.

例
椅子いすを六つ持ち上げてください。
Please, hand me six chairs.

And again, つ is used as general-purpose counter and is good mechanism if we don't know certain counter. Alas, it is limited to 10 and if we get unlucky enough to not know counter and have number 26, クソ.

Sino-Japanese Reading

Sino-Japanese reading is basically 音読み of given number character. It is reading borrowed from China and is native reading for Kanji.

例
椅子を六脚ろっきゃく持ち上げてください。
Please, hand me six chairs.

説明
脚 (きゃく) is counter for chairs or seats.
